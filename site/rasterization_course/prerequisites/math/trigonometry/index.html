Trigonometry

TL;DR - What should you know?

  • Angles are specified in radians. An angle in degrees α\alphaα is related to one in radians bbb as α360∘=b2π\frac{\alpha}{360^\circ} = \frac{b}{2\pi}360∘α​=2πb​
  • In a unit circle, how do you draw/read the sine and cosine of an angle α\alphaα?
  • What is sin⁡2(α)+cos⁡2(α)\sin^2(\alpha) + \cos^2(\alpha)sin2(α)+cos2(α)?
  • Where do sine and cosine take on special values (1,0,−11,0,-11,0,−1)?
  • Where are the sine and cosine positive and negative?
  • What is the tangent? Knowing sine and cosine is important in all kinds of contexts. For a lot of applications related to geometry, you will most likely only need two pieces of information:
  • In a triangle with a right-angle, where do you find the sine and cosine?
  • How do they look as a graph (where are some special values located)?

The easiest thing to keep in mind is thinking about a circle with radius 111. Choose some angle α\alphaα (alpha). Draw a line which has that angle counter-clockwise from the xxx-axis through the origin O\mathbf{O}O. The line meets the circle in a point P\mathbf{P}P. Draw a straight line through P\mathbf{P}P parallel to the yyy-axis, which will intersect the xxx-axis in a point Q\mathbf{Q}Q. You now have a right triangle ΔOQP\Delta \mathbf{O}\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{P} ΔOQP, where the right angle occurs at the point Q\mathbf{Q}Q.

The sine sin⁡\sinsin is just the yyy-segment from Q\mathbf{Q}Q to P\mathbf{P}P. It is positive, if the line goes up and negative if it goes down.

The cosine cos⁡\coscos is just the xxx-segment from O\mathbf{O}O to Q\mathbf{Q}Q. It is positive, if the line goes right and negative if it goes left.

The segment from O\mathbf{O}O to P\mathbf{P}P is called the hypotenuse and its length is equal to the circle radius, which is 111 in our case.

Below you can see a visualization of the sine and cosine. You can move around the point on the circle. This changes the angle α\alphaα, showing you how it relates to the different values of sin⁡\sinsin and cos⁡\coscos.

In the visualization you will also see to additional values: The tangent (tan⁡\tantan) and the cotangent (cot⁡\cotcot). These are not as important as the sine and cosine, especially the cotangent, but the tangent is encountered pretty often. In the visualization you can see that its value is the height (positive or negative) of where the line through the angle vector intersects the y-axis at x=1x=1x=1. They can be defined as follows:

tan⁡(α)=sin⁡(α)cos⁡(α)cot⁡(α)=cos⁡(α)sin⁡(α)\begin{aligned} \tan(\alpha) &= \frac{\sin(\alpha)}{\cos(\alpha)} \\ \cot(\alpha) &= \frac{\cos(\alpha)}{\sin(\alpha)} \end{aligned} tan(α)cot(α)​=cos(α)sin(α)​=sin(α)cos(α)​​

If the circle didn't have radius r=1r=1r=1, but instead just some rrr, the whole triangle would look exactly the same, but scaled. So the side lengths would become rsin⁡(α)r\sin(\alpha)rsin(α) and rcos⁡(α)r\cos(\alpha)rcos(α).

From the Pythagorean theorem, we know that the sum of the squared shorter sides of a right triangle is equal to the squared length of the longer hypotenuse. Since we have a right triangle and used a unit circle with r=1r=1r=1, we have the important property:

sin⁡2(α)+cos⁡2(α)=1\begin{aligned} \sin^2(\alpha) + \cos^2(\alpha) = 1 \end{aligned} sin2(α)+cos2(α)=1​

This comes up quite often, so it is important to memorize.

Another important thing to remember, is that in general, angles are specified in radians, not degrees, so it is good to get familiar with it. Radians measure the length of an arc segment on the unit circle, that is covered by an angle. If you can't remember how to switch between them, here is a simple way to remember it. Degrees and radians are proportional to each other. The circumference of the unit circle is 2π2\pi2π. A circle has 360∘360^\circ360∘. Dividing both angles by their respective full circle will yield the same ratio (a half circle is a half circle). With that we can relate an angle in degree α\alphaα and in radians bbb as:

α360∘=b2πα=360∘b2π=180∘bπb=2πα360∘=πα180∘\begin{aligned} \frac{\alpha}{360^\circ} &= \frac{b}{2\pi} \\ \alpha &= 360^\circ\frac{b}{2\pi} \\ &= 180^\circ \frac{b}{\pi} \\ b &= \frac{2\pi\alpha}{360^\circ}\\ &= \frac{\pi\alpha}{180^\circ} \end{aligned} 360∘α​αb​=2πb​=360∘2πb​=180∘πb​=360∘2πα​=180∘πα​​

From the visualization above, you can deduce a number of special angle-number pairs, which are good to know:

sin⁡(0∘)=sin⁡(0)=0cos⁡(0∘)=cos⁡(0)=1sin⁡(90∘)=sin⁡(π2)=1cos⁡(90∘)=cos⁡(π2)=0sin⁡(180∘)=sin⁡(π)=0cos⁡(180∘)=cos⁡(π)=−1sin⁡(270∘)=sin⁡(3π2)=−1cos⁡(270∘)=cos⁡(3π2)=0\begin{aligned} \sin(0^\circ) &= \sin(0) = 0\\ \cos(0^\circ) &= \cos(0) = 1\\ \sin(90^\circ) &= \sin(\frac{\pi}{2}) = 1\\ \cos(90^\circ) &= \cos(\frac{\pi}{2}) = 0 \\ \sin(180^\circ) &= \sin(\pi) = 0\\ \cos(180^\circ) &= \cos(\pi) = -1 \\ \sin(270^\circ) &= \sin(\frac{3\pi}{2}) = -1\\ \cos(270^\circ) &= \cos(\frac{3\pi}{2}) = 0 \\ \end{aligned} sin(0∘)cos(0∘)sin(90∘)cos(90∘)sin(180∘)cos(180∘)sin(270∘)cos(270∘)​=sin(0)=0=cos(0)=1=sin(2π​)=1=cos(2π​)=0=sin(π)=0=cos(π)=−1=sin(23π​)=−1=cos(23π​)=0​

The diagonal angles (45∘,135∘,225∘,315∘45^\circ, 135^\circ, 225^\circ, 315^\circ45∘,135∘,225∘,315∘) also have a trick to remember them. In those cases the right triangle is symmetric and so the sin⁡\sinsin and cos⁡\coscos sides must have the same length, let's call it lll. We have sin⁡2+cos⁡2=1⇒l2+l2=2l2=1⇒l=(12)\sin^2 + \cos^2 = 1 \Rightarrow l^2 + l^2 = 2l^2 = 1 \Rightarrow l = \sqrt(\frac{1}{2})sin2+cos2=1⇒l2+l2=2l2=1⇒l=(​21​). You just have to add a negative sign to this length, whenever the sine goes downward or the cosine left.
